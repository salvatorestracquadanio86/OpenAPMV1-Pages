 Over the last 10 years, I have worked in the field of Asset Performance Management, both from a technical and a functional perspective. I have had the opportunity to gain hands-on experience with some of the most advanced platforms available on the market and to participate in projects where reliability, maintenance, and asset management were critical business processes.

Throughout this journey, I have developed a conviction: large organizations have access to extraordinary tools. There are highly sophisticated solutions that enable companies to build structured processes, collect and analyze data, support decision-making, and continuously improve asset performance.

But what happens when we move into the world of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs)?

Very often, we encounter organizations facing the same challenges:

    maintenance activities that are difficult to track;
    critical knowledge concentrated in the minds of a few individuals;
    documentation scattered across folders, emails, and spreadsheets;
    processes that exist in practice but not within a structured system.

Yet the underlying need remains exactly the same: understanding assets, preserving their value, and managing them in a more structured and informed way.

These reflections led to the idea of OpenAPM, a personal project that I am pursuing out of passion and long-standing interest in a field in which I have been working for many years.

The term "Open" does not refer to Open Source Software. To me, it means something different. It means envisioning a platform that is open to a wide variety of organizations.

Open to companies with complex operational structures that need a flexible foundation on which to build tailored processes.

Open to organizations that would like to introduce Asset Performance Management practices but lack the financial or organizational resources required for large-scale enterprise projects.

The goal is not to replace or compete with the major players in the industry, which continue to represent an important benchmark for the market. Their expertise, capabilities, and the value they deliver to large organizations are beyond question. Rather, the goal is to explore whether there is room for a solution that makes these concepts more accessible, more gradual to adopt, and closer to everyday operational needs.

Another aspect I consider fundamental is simplicity.

Many organizations do not have in-house specialists in Asset Performance Management or advanced maintenance methodologies. For this reason, I believe that future systems should be able to guide users through processes, models, and decisions, helping them understand not only what to do, but also why they are doing it. Templates, guided workflows, and AI-powered assistance could help bridge the gap between industry best practices and organizations that currently lack access to specialized expertise.

I would also like to clarify another important point. OpenAPM is a personal and independent initiative, with no short-term commercial objectives. The current vision is to make the majority of its functionality available free of charge, allowing professionals and organizations to experiment, evaluate, and contribute ideas and feedback. Should anyone in the future require significant customizations, specific developments, or consulting activities beyond the project's natural roadmap, these may be considered separately. However, they are not the primary purpose of the initiative today.

Before defining features, priorities, and a roadmap, I would like to hear from people who deal with these challenges on a daily basis.

How are assets, maintenance activities, and operational knowledge managed in your organization today?

Do you use dedicated tools, or do you still rely on spreadsheets, general-purpose ERP systems, or internal procedures?

And most importantly: what is the first problem you would like to solve?

If you work in this field, I would be happy to connect in the comments or via direct message.

And if you are not directly involved but know companies, maintenance managers, reliability engineers, facility managers, or other professionals who might be interested in this discussion, I would greatly appreciate it if you shared this post with them. 

At this stage, feedback from people who face these challenges every day is more valuable than anything else.
